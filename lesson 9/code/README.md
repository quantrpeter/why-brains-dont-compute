# Lesson 9: Spatial Intervals

Perceived line lengths often differ from physical measurements. The same retinal projection can arise from different physical lengths and orientations; empirical ranking based on natural scene statistics predicts these discrepancies.

## Key Concepts

### Ambiguous Retinal Projections
The same retinal projection—a line of a given length and orientation on the retina—can arise from many different physical configurations. A short line close to the eye and a long line far away can produce identical projections. The brain cannot uniquely invert the projection to recover physical length; it must rely on the statistics of past experience.

### Laser-Scanned Frequency Data
Laser scanning of natural scenes provides data on how often different line projections occur. Surfaces in depth are foreshortened; gravity limits vertical extension, so short vertical projections are more frequent than short horizontal ones. Template sampling of line projections yields frequency distributions that can be ranked empirically.

### The Vertical-Horizontal Illusion
Vertical lines look longer than horizontal ones of equal physical length; the effect is maximal at ~30° from vertical. This matches the empirical ranking: vertical projections of a given retinal length correspond to less frequent (and thus "longer") physical sources than horizontal projections. The frequency scale predicts psychophysical results.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why does foreshortening and gravity lead to different frequency distributions for vertical vs horizontal line projections?
2. How does empirical ranking explain the vertical-horizontal illusion without invoking "errors" in perception?
3. What would it mean for perception to be "correct" if the same retinal input is ambiguous?

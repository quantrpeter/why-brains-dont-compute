# Lesson 17: Object Size

Classical size "illusions"—the Ebbinghaus effect, size-distance paradox, and Moon illusion—are explained empirically by the statistics of natural scene geometry. Monocular cues (perspective, occlusion, aerial perspective, motion parallax) contribute to the frequency structure that shapes perceived size.

## Key Concepts

### Ebbinghaus Size-Contrast Effect
In the Ebbinghaus illusion, a target circle surrounded by larger circles appears smaller than an identical target surrounded by smaller circles. Traditional accounts invoke contrast or assimilation. The empirical explanation: the frequency of occurrence of projected sizes in natural scenes, given surrounding context, predicts the effect. Objects flanked by larger neighbors tend to be smaller in physical space; the opposite holds for objects flanked by smaller neighbors. Perception reflects these statistics.

### Size-Distance Paradox and Monocular Cues
The same retinal projection can arise from a small nearby object or a large distant one. Monocular cues—perspective, occlusion, aerial perspective, motion parallax—provide information about depth and scale. The size-distance paradox: background objects with the same physical size as foreground objects often appear larger because, in natural scenes, objects that project at a given size while being farther away tend to be physically larger. Empirical rank on this frequency scale predicts perceived size.

### Moon Illusion
The Moon appears larger near the horizon than at the zenith, though its retinal image is the same. The empirical explanation: the horizon Moon is seen in the context of diminished terrestrial projections (trees, buildings) that make it rank higher among "large" projections. The zenith Moon, seen against the empty sky, ranks lower. Perception reflects the relative frequency of such projections in experience, not a computation of angular size.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does the Ebbinghaus effect challenge the idea that we perceive "actual" object size?
2. Why might the size-distance paradox be ecologically useful—when would it help to perceive distant objects as larger?
3. How does the Moon illusion illustrate that context (surrounding elements) shapes perception through empirical ranking?

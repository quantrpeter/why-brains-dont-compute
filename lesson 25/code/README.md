# Lesson 25: Summing Up

A final recap of the book's central argument: brains do not compute. Animals cannot measure reality. Perception and behavior are shaped by the frequency of occurrence of stimulus patterns in experience. This has implications for neuroscience, artificial intelligence, and the philosophy of mind.

## Key Concepts

### Brains Don't Compute
The book's central thesis: biological nervous systems do not perform algorithmic computation. They do not take measurements of the physical world, apply rules, and derive representations. Instead, they operate empirically—building associations between recurring stimulus patterns and successful behaviors through evolution, learning, and culture. What we perceive and how we act reflect accumulated experience, not computed models of reality.

### Perception Shaped by Frequency of Occurrence
Across domains—geometry, lightness, color, motion, size, stereopsis—perception is predicted by the frequency of occurrence of stimulus patterns in natural scenes. Empirical ranking explains systematic discrepancies between physical measurements and subjective experience. The inverse problem (ambiguous stimuli) precludes direct measurement; the empirical strategy circumvents it by linking inputs to outputs that have been rewarded by experience.

### Implications for Neuroscience, AI, and Philosophy
For neuroscience: the goal shifts from finding the "algorithm" of perception to understanding how empirical associations are built and implemented. For AI: success in narrow tasks does not imply that brains compute; biological intelligence may rely on different principles. For philosophy: the gap between objective reality and subjective experience is not a bug to be fixed but a consequence of how empirical systems work—they succeed by utility, not by veridical representation.

## Discussion Questions

1. How would you summarize the empirical alternative to computational theories of perception in one paragraph?
2. What implications does "brains don't compute" have for how we should design artificial intelligence systems?
3. If perception is not a representation of reality, what is it—and why has it been so successful for survival?

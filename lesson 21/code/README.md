# Lesson 21: Mechanisms

Neural plasticity—the ability of connections to change with experience—implements empirical learning. Short-term changes (facilitation, depression), long-term potentiation and depression, and reward systems (dopamine) provide the mechanistic substrate for building and refining stimulus–behavior associations.

## Key Concepts

### Neural Plasticity
The nervous system is not fixed; synapses and circuits can change in response to activity and experience. This plasticity is the biological basis for learning. Hebb's principle—"neurons that fire together wire together"—captures the idea that co-activation strengthens connections. Plasticity operates at multiple timescales, from milliseconds to years, allowing both rapid adaptation and enduring memory.

### Short-Term and Long-Term Changes
Short-term changes include synaptic facilitation (increased transmitter release with repeated stimulation) and depression (decreased release). These last seconds to minutes. Long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD) involve structural and molecular changes that persist for hours, days, or longer. LTP and LTD are thought to underlie learning and memory. The transition from short- to long-term changes often requires repeated or salient stimulation—consistent with trial-and-error learning.

### Reward Systems and Dopamine
Dopaminergic systems signal reward and prediction error: when outcomes are better than expected, dopamine is released; when worse, activity decreases. This provides a reinforcement signal that guides trial-and-error learning—telling the brain which associations to strengthen. Reward-based learning is central to the empirical strategy: success reinforces; failure does not. The mechanism is not computation of optimal policy but gradual adjustment driven by feedback.

2. Why might both LTP and LTD be necessary for adaptive learning?
3. In what way does dopamine implement "credit assignment" for empirical learning?

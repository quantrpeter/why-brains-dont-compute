# Lesson 14: Color Psychophysics

Complex colorimetric functions—just noticeable differences (JNDs) and the Bezold-Brücke effect—can be predicted from spectral statistics of natural scenes, extending the empirical framework to fine-grained color perception.

## Key Concepts

### Just Noticeable Differences (JNDs)
JNDs measure the smallest change in wavelength (or other color parameters) that an observer can detect. The resulting discrimination function is complex and varies nonlinearly across the spectrum. Rather than arising from fixed physiological mechanisms, this function is predicted by the frequency of occurrence of spectral patterns in natural scenes—regions where small changes are more common in the environment correspond to finer discrimination.

### Bezold-Brücke Effect
The Bezold-Brücke effect describes how perceived hue shifts with intensity: at low luminance, short wavelengths appear bluer, while at high luminance the same wavelengths appear greener. This phenomenon has long puzzled color scientists. The empirical explanation holds that the co-occurrence of spectral correlates (wavelength and intensity) in natural scenes predicts these shifts—what we perceive reflects the statistical structure of our visual experience, not a direct mapping of physical spectra.

### Spectral Statistics and Colorimetric Functions
Spectral statistics from natural scenes—obtained by sampling real-world environments—predict not only basic color appearance but also the complex psychophysical functions that describe discrimination and constancy. This supports the view that color perception is built from accumulated experience with recurring spectral patterns rather than from computations over physical measurements.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does the empirical prediction of JNDs challenge the idea that color discrimination reflects fixed neural "tuning" to wavelengths?
2. Why might the Bezold-Brücke effect be biologically useful rather than a flaw in the visual system?
3. What would it mean for color science if all major colorimetric functions could be derived from natural scene statistics?

# Lesson 13: Color

Color vision is an empirical construct—created by brains for biological advantage—not a direct reflection of physical spectra. Color space, unique hues, constancy, and contrast are explained by the frequency of occurrence of spectral patterns in natural scenes.

## Key Concepts

### Color Space and Unique Hues
Color can be described by hue, saturation, and lightness. Four primary categories—unique red, green, blue, and yellow—anchor our color experience; we do not see "reddish-green" or "bluish-yellow." Color space is organized by these perceptual primitives, which correspond to patterns of cone activation and their statistical co-occurrence in natural scenes.

### Color Constancy and Color Contrast
Color constancy: surfaces keep their apparent color despite changes in illumination (e.g., a white shirt looks white indoors and in sunlight). Color contrast: identical spectra appear different in different surrounds—a gray patch can look tinted by the surrounding color. Neither effect is fully explained by adaptation; both reflect the empirical statistics of spectral patterns. The frequency of occurrence of spectral correlates predicts constancy and contrast.

### Empirical Explanation of Color Perception
Spectral statistics in natural scenes—how often particular wavelength combinations occur in particular contexts—predict hue, saturation, and brightness perception. Color is not "out there" in the world; it is a construction that has been shaped by evolution and experience to support behavior. The empirical framework unifies color constancy, contrast, and psychophysics under a single principle.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why do we have four unique hues (red, green, blue, yellow) rather than a continuous spectrum of primitives?
2. How does color constancy pose a challenge for theories that assume perception computes physical properties?
3. In what sense is color "created by brains" rather than "detected" from the environment?

# Lesson 24: Statistical Inference

Bayesian approaches to perception use probability to infer the most likely source of a stimulus. While influential, they face fundamental problems: unknowable priors and the assumption of a computable world. Empirical ranking offers an alternative that does not require computation or explicit priors.

## Key Concepts

### Bayes' Theorem and Perception
Bayes' theorem combines prior probability (what we expect) with likelihood (how well the stimulus matches each possible source) to yield posterior probability (what we infer). Applied to perception: given a retinal image, the brain infers the most likely physical cause. This seems to explain why perception is probabilistic and context-dependent. Bayesian models have been applied to many perceptual phenomena.

### Problems with Bayesian Approaches
Bayesian perception requires a prior—the probability distribution over possible physical sources. For biology, this prior is unknowable: organisms have no access to the true statistics of the world, only to their own experience. Priors are often assumed or fitted to data, which risks circularity. Moreover, Bayesian inference assumes that the world can be described by computable probability distributions—a strong assumption that may not hold for the inverse problems that perception faces.

### Empirical Ranking as Alternative
Empirical ranking does not compute posteriors or use explicit priors. Instead, perception reflects the rank of the current stimulus on a scale defined by the frequency of occurrence of similar patterns in past experience. No probability computation is needed; the association between stimulus and response is built directly by evolution and learning. This avoids the need for unknowable priors and fits the evidence that perception is shaped by frequency of occurrence in natural scenes.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why is the "prior" in Bayesian perception problematic for a biological system?
2. How does empirical ranking differ from Bayesian inference in what it requires the brain to do?
3. Could Bayesian and empirical approaches be reconciled, or are they fundamentally incompatible?

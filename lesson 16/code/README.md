# Lesson 16: Motion Direction

Aperture effects—how framing changes perceived motion direction—are predicted by the frequency of occluded line projections in natural scenes. The circular aperture (orthogonal bias) and vertical slit (barber pole effect) both follow from this empirical logic.

## Key Concepts

### Circular Aperture and Orthogonal Bias
When a moving line is viewed through a circular aperture, observers typically perceive motion orthogonal to the line's orientation rather than along it. This "aperture problem" has been explained computationally as the brain solving for the true motion. The empirical alternative: the frequency of projected directions through circular occlusion in natural scenes predicts this bias. Shorter line segments that fill the aperture orthogonally occur more often than longer segments aligned with motion.

### Barber Pole Effect
Through a vertical slit, horizontal motion appears vertical—the classic barber pole illusion. The same logic applies: the minimum line length that fills a vertical aperture orthogonally is a vertical segment. In natural scenes, vertical slits (e.g., gaps between trees, doorways) are common, and the frequency of projected directions through such apertures predicts that horizontal motion will be perceived as vertical. The empirical framework unifies these seemingly disparate phenomena.

### Minimum Line Length Explanation
The key insight is that shorter lines are more frequent in natural scenes when occlusion creates apertures. The visual system has accumulated experience with these statistics. Perceived direction reflects the empirical rank of projected motion directions given the aperture shape—not a geometric computation of "true" motion from ambiguous local signals.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does the aperture problem illustrate that motion direction, like speed, is underdetermined by the retinal image?
2. Why might the barber pole effect be ecologically relevant—when would perceiving vertical motion through a vertical slit be advantageous?
3. How does the minimum line length argument connect to the idea that perception reflects frequency of occurrence rather than physical reality?

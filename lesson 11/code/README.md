# Lesson 11: Lightness and Darkness

Lightness perception is not proportional to luminance. Simultaneous lightness contrast and Gelb's room demonstrate that context—implied illumination and surrounding surfaces—determines perceived lightness through experience, not retinal receptive fields.

## Key Concepts

### Luminance vs Lightness
Luminance is the physical measure of light reaching the eye; lightness is the perceived reflectance or "how light or dark" a surface appears. The two do not map one-to-one. Identical luminance can produce different lightness percepts depending on context. Simultaneous lightness (or brightness) contrast—where a gray patch looks darker on a light background and lighter on a dark background—illustrates this dissociation.

### Why Receptive Field Explanations Fail
A common physiological explanation invokes retinal ganglion cell receptive fields: center-surround antagonism could make a gray patch appear darker when surrounded by light. Gelb's room refutes this: a black paper lit by a hidden spotlight appears white until context is revealed (e.g., a white card introduced), at which point the same retinal input is perceived as black. Context—implied illumination—changes appearance without changing the retinal image. Receptive fields alone cannot explain this.

### Experience Determines Lightness
Experience with natural surfaces—how often particular luminance patterns co-occur with particular reflectances and illuminations—determines perceived lightness values. The brain uses the statistics of past experience to interpret ambiguous retinal input. Lightness is an empirical construct, not a direct readout of luminance.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why does Gelb's room undermine the receptive field explanation of lightness perception?
2. How does "implied illumination" differ from the actual light reaching the retina?
3. What would it mean for lightness to be determined by the "frequency of occurrence" of luminance patterns?

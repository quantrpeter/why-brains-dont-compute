# Lesson 1: Solving Problems

Biological agents solve problems either by logical rules (algorithmic) or empirically by trial-and-error. Intelligence is not primarily logical, and AI success does not imply that brains compute.

## Key Concepts

### Algorithmic vs Empirical Problem Solving
Algorithmic approaches use explicit logical rules to derive solutions, while empirical approaches rely on trial-and-error refinement rewarded by success. The segregation between computer scientists and biologically inclined neuroscientists often stems from which paradigm they favor. Most biological problem-solving—including perception and behavior—operates empirically rather than algorithmically.

### The Monty Hall Problem
The Monty Hall problem illustrates that logical thinking is not a human forte: most people fail to update probabilities correctly when a door is revealed. Similar biases appear in multiplication and other reasoning tasks. This suggests that evolution favored empirical strategies over formal logic for survival.

### AI and the Brain
Artificial intelligence can succeed at tasks like chess or image recognition without implying that brains compute in the same way. AI systems lack the phenomenological goals and biological constraints that shape real brains. The cart-before-the-horse risk is assuming that because computers solve problems, brains must work like computers.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why might evolution have favored empirical over algorithmic problem-solving for most biological tasks?
2. How does the Monty Hall problem reveal the limits of human logical reasoning?
3. If AI can outperform humans at certain tasks, does that mean brains compute? Why or why not?

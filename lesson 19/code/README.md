# Lesson 19: Stimuli and Behavior

Stimuli are not raw physical energy but are filtered and transformed before reaching the brain. Physiologists and psychologists approach the stimulus–behavior link differently, but both can be unified under the empirical framework that links recurring input patterns to successful responses.

## Key Concepts

### What Stimuli Really Are
Stimuli are not the physical energy "out there" but the patterns that reach the nervous system after pre-neural filtering (e.g., the optics of the eye, the mechanical properties of the ear). Higher-order processing involves feedback loops that further shape what counts as a stimulus. The link between physical reality and neural input is complex and non-linear—reinforcing the view that perception cannot be a simple measurement of the environment.

### Physiologist vs Psychologist Perspectives
Physiologists focus on neural mechanisms: reflexes, motor control, circuitry, and the causal chain from sensory input to motor output. Psychologists focus on subjective reports, psychophysics, and cognition—the experienced relationship between stimuli and behavior. The two fields use different methods and levels of description, but both seek to understand how stimuli lead to behavior.

### The Common Empirical Strategy
Despite their differences, both fields implicitly or explicitly rely on linking stimuli (as processed by the organism) to responses. The empirical framework unifies them: associations between recurring stimulus patterns and successful behaviors are built by evolution, lifetime learning, and culture. Neither physiology nor psychology requires the assumption that the brain computes representations of physical reality—only that it links inputs to outputs in ways that have been rewarded by experience.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why does it matter that "stimuli" are not identical to physical energy? What implications does this have for perception?
2. How might a physiologist and a psychologist disagree about what counts as an "explanation" of behavior?
3. In what way does the empirical strategy provide common ground between neural mechanisms and subjective experience?

# Lesson 15: Motion Speed

Physical motion is conflated by 3D-to-2D projection onto the retina, and perceived speed—including the flash-lag effect—can be explained empirically by the frequency of occurrence of projected motion sequences in natural experience.

## Key Concepts

### The Inverse Problem of Motion
The same sequence of retinal images can arise from many different 3D motions in the world. Speed and direction are conflated when 3D motion is projected onto the 2D retina. The visible range of motion is roughly 0.1–175°/s, but within that range, physical speed cannot be uniquely recovered from the image. This ambiguity is another instance of the inverse problem that pervades perception.

### Flash-Lag Effect
When a moving stimulus and a stationary flash are presented at the same physical location, the moving stimulus appears ahead of the flash—as if it had "led" the flash. The effect increases with speed. Traditional explanations invoked neural delays or extrapolation. The empirical approach shows that the frequency of occurrence of projected motion sequences in natural scenes predicts this psychometric function: what we perceive reflects the statistical structure of our experience with moving objects.

### Virtual Environment Simulation
To test the empirical hypothesis, researchers simulate moving objects in virtual environments that approximate the statistics of natural scenes. By sampling the frequency of image sequences corresponding to different physical motions, they can predict perceived speed. This approach extends the empirical framework from static stimuli (geometry, color, lightness) to dynamic perception.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why can't the visual system simply "measure" the speed of motion on the retina? What information is lost in 3D-to-2D projection?
2. How does the flash-lag effect illustrate that perception is not a direct readout of the current retinal image?
3. In what ways might the empirical explanation of motion speed differ from a computational model that tries to estimate physical velocity?

# Lesson 23: Feature Detection

The feature-detection theory—from Hubel & Wiesel's edge and orientation detectors to "grandmother cells"—proposes that neurons encode increasingly specific stimuli. While influential, this framework has limitations when understood as a complete account of perception and neural function.

## Key Concepts

### Hubel & Wiesel Feature Detectors
Hubel and Wiesel discovered that neurons in primary visual cortex respond to edges, orientations, and motion direction—not to spots of light like retinal ganglion cells. These "feature detectors" were interpreted as building blocks: the visual system decomposes the image into simple features and reassembles them. This view fit the computational paradigm: the brain analyzes the input to extract meaningful structure.

### Increasingly Specific Neurons
Beyond V1, neurons respond to more complex and specific stimuli: face cells in inferotemporal cortex, place cells in hippocampus. The "grandmother cell" idea—a neuron that fires only for one specific person or object—represents the extreme. Lesions of inferotemporal cortex impair object recognition in monkeys, suggesting that these areas are critical for perception. The hierarchy of feature detectors seemed to support a representational view: the brain builds increasingly abstract representations of the world.

### Limitations of the Feature Detection Framework
Feature detection can be seen as a modern reflex arc: stimulus → detector → response. But it faces problems. The inverse problem remains: features are ambiguous; they cannot uniquely specify their physical sources. Neurons may reflect statistical regularities in input (empirical associations) rather than "detecting" features in a computational sense. The framework also struggles to explain how perception arises from neural activity—the hard problem of consciousness—and why perception often deviates from physical measurements in systematic ways predicted by empirical ranking.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does the feature-detection hierarchy (edges → orientations → faces) relate to the idea that the brain "computes" a representation of the world?
2. Why might "grandmother cells" be better understood as neurons that have learned strong associations with particular stimuli rather than as literal detectors?
3. In what ways does the empirical framework offer an alternative to the feature-detection account of perception?

# Lesson 8: What We Perceive

Traditional theories assume perception constructs representations of physical reality. The inverse problem undermines this view; the empirical alternative proposes that brains accumulate experience and refine stimulus-response links by trial-and-error.

## Key Concepts

### Traditional Assumptions: Marr's Computational Theory
Marr's framework held that vision proceeds through detection, encoding, and sparse representation—building a description of the world from retinal input. The goal was to recover physical properties (depth, shape, reflectance) by computation. This view dominated vision science for decades.

### The Dilemma: The Inverse Problem
Stimuli are ambiguous. The same retinal image can arise from infinitely many physical configurations. Features cannot be uniquely mapped back to reality; the inverse problem is underdetermined. No algorithm can compute "the" correct interpretation from retinal input alone. This undermines the computational theory of perception.

### The Empirical Solution
Brains accumulate experience over evolution and lifetime. Trial-and-error refines the links between stimuli and responses; success (survival, reward) reinforces useful associations. Perception starts at chance and improves with experience. The mechanism may not be revealed by analyzing single neurons—the relevant level is the statistics of co-occurrence and the feedback loop to behavior.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why does the inverse problem make it impossible for perception to "compute" physical reality?
2. How does the empirical framework differ from Marr's computational theory in explaining what we perceive?
3. What caveats apply to the empirical view—e.g., starting at chance, improving with experience?

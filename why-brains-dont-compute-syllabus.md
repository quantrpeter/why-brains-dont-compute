# Why Brains Don't Compute: Video Lesson Syllabus

By Dale Purves — Duke Institute for Brain Sciences

---

## Part I — Solving Problems in Different Realities

### Lesson 1: Solving Problems

**Goal:** Introduce the central argument: biological agents solve problems either by logical rules or empirically by trial-and-error.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Two Perspectives | Algorithmic (rule-based) vs empirical (trial-and-error) problem solving |
| Consequences | The segregation of computer scientists and biologically inclined neuroscientists |
| Intelligence | Why logical thinking is not a human forte (Monty Hall Problem, multiplication biases) |
| Artificial Intelligence | AI success does not mean brains compute; missing phenomenological goals |
| Common Ground | Definitions of "problem" and "machine"; the cart-before-the-horse risk |

**Further Readings:**
- Bowler PJ (2003) *Evolution: the history of an idea*
- Gleick J (2011) *The information*
- Gould SJ (1977) *Ever since Darwin*
- Harari YN (2014) *Sapiens: a brief history of humankind*

---

### Lesson 2: Objective and Subjective Reality

**Goal:** Explain the gap between physical (objective) reality and perceived (subjective) experience, and why animals cannot measure the environment.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Newtonian Reality | Classical physics as the relevant frame; chaos and determinism |
| Biological Measurements | Animals lack instruments; the inverse problem (conflated stimulus sources) |
| What We Perceive | Berkeley's insight — perception exists in our heads, not in the physical world |
| Discrepancies | Systematic mismatches between measurements and perceptions (e.g., retinal projection) |
| What About Machines | Could non-biological machines perceive the world the way we do? |

**Further Readings:**
- Dawkins R (1986) *The blind watchmaker*
- Dobzhansky T (1973) Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution
- Purves D (2010) *Brains: how they seem to work*

**Goal:** Review the history and principles of algorithmic computation from the abacus to modern electronic computers.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Instantiation in Machines | Abacus, Pascal's calculator, Babbage's Difference Engine, Ada Lovelace |

**Further Readings:**
- Ceruzzi PE (2003) *A history of modern computing*
- Gleick J (2011) *The information*
- Turing A (1936) On computable numbers

### Lesson 4: Coding

**Goal:** Contrast computer coding with neural coding; explore whether the concept of a "code" applies to nervous systems.

| Topic | Details |

## Part III — Neural Networks

### Lesson 5: Neural Networks

**Goal:** Trace the origins of artificial neural networks from McCulloch & Pitts to perceptrons, and understand their initial failure.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| History | McCulloch & Pitts (1943) — problems solved by modifiable network connections |
| Key Attribute | Trial-and-error learning rewarded by success; no need to know the answer |
| A Problem for ANNs | The "credit assignment" challenge — how to provide feedback |
| Failure to Take Off | Algorithmic computing's dominance; neural nets were slow and inscrutable |
| Perceptrons | Rosenblatt's two-layer classifier; Minsky & Papert's XOR limitation (1969) |

**Further Readings:**
- McCulloch WS, Pitts W (1943) A logical calculus of the ideas immanent in nervous activity
- Minsky M, Papert S (1972) *Perceptrons*
- Russel S, Norvig SJ (2003) *Artificial intelligence: a modern approach*

---

### Lesson 6: Resurrection of Neural Networks

**Goal:** Understand how backpropagation revived neural networks, and why unsupervised learning and large search spaces favour empirical strategies.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Error Correction by Back Propagation | Werbos (1974), Rumelhart & McClelland (1986); gradient descent |
| Biological Implausibility | Back prop requires known answers and recurrent weight specification |
| Unsupervised Credit Assignment | Evolution as the "supervisor"; genetic algorithms and reward |
| Search Spaces | Small spaces (~10^10) suit algorithms; huge spaces (~10^100+) demand empirical approaches |

**Further Readings:**
- Nielsen MA (2015) How the backpropagation algorithm works
- Rumelhart DE, McClelland JL (1986) *Parallel distributed processing*
- Werbos P (1974) *The roots of backpropagation* (PhD thesis, Harvard)

---

### Lesson 7: Learning Empirically

**Goal:** Show how game-playing vindicates empirical learning — from Rubik's Cube to AlphaZero — and its implications for neuroscience.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| An Algorithmic Beginning | Rubik's Cube (~10^19 states) solved by a simple algorithm in 20 steps |
| Games that Cannot Be Won Algorithmically | Game tree complexity: tic-tac-toe (10^4), checkers (10^20), chess (10^120), Go (10^320) |
| Deep Blue vs AlphaZero | Brute force + expert moves vs pure self-play reinforcement learning |
| DeepMind's Success | AlphaGo, AlphaZero, MuZero — beating champions via trial-and-error |
| Implications for Neuroscience | Real-life search spaces dwarf board games; brains operate empirically |

**Further Readings:**
- Silver D et al (2017) Mastering the game of Go without human knowledge
- Purves D (2019) What does AI's success playing complex board games tell brain scientists?
- Saxe A et al (2021) If deep learning is the answer, what is the question?

## Part IV — Perception

### Lesson 8: What We Perceive

**Goal:** Challenge the traditional assumption that perception constructs representations of physical reality; propose the empirical alternative.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Traditional Assumptions | Neural detection → encoding → sparse representation (Marr's computational theory) |
| The Dilemma | Stimuli are ambiguous (inverse problem); features cannot map back to reality |
| A Solution | Brains accumulate experience; trial-and-error refines stimulus-response links |
| Caveats | Starts at chance level; improves with experience; analysis may not reveal the mechanism |

**Further Readings:**
- Berkeley G (1709) *A new theory of vision*
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*
- Rock I (1995) *Perception*

---

### Lesson 9: Spatial Intervals

**Goal:** Explain why perceived line lengths differ from physical measurements, using frequency-of-occurrence data from natural scenes.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Discrepancies | Same retinal projection can arise from different physical lengths/orientations |
| Determining Frequencies | Laser-scanned scenes; template sampling of line projections |
| An Example | Vertical lines look longer than horizontal; maximum at ~30° from vertical |
| Explanation | Surfaces in depth are foreshortened; gravity limits vertical extension → more short vertical projections |
| Empirical Ranking | Rank on frequency scale predicts psychophysical results |

**Further Readings:**
- Howe CQ, Purves D (2005) *Perceiving geometry*
- Howe CQ, Purves D (2005) The Müller-Lyer illusion explained by the statistics of image-source relationships

---

### Lesson 10: Angles

**Goal:** Show how the systematic over/underestimation of angles is predicted by the frequency of angle projections in natural scenes.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Perceived Angles | Acute angles are overestimated; obtuse ones are underestimated |
| Frequency of Angle Sources | Laser scanning reveals 90° projections are least frequent |
| Reason | Right-angle sources require larger planar surfaces, which are rarer |
| Systematic Misestimation | Empirical rank of projected angles predicts psychophysical data |
| Alternative Explanations | Eye anatomy, lateral inhibition — none supported by evidence |

**Further Readings:**
- Howe CQ, Purves D (2005) Natural scene geometry predicts the perception of angles and line orientation
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 5

---

### Lesson 11: Lightness and Darkness

**Goal:** Demonstrate that lightness/brightness perception is not proportional to luminance, and explore the empirical explanation.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Seeing in Black and White | Luminance vs lightness; simultaneous lightness/brightness contrast |
| A Possible Physiological Explanation | Retinal ganglion cell receptive fields and surround inhibition |
| Why That Explanation Fails | Gelb's room — context (implied illumination) changes appearance |
| A Different Approach | Experience with natural surfaces determines perceived lightness values |

**Further Readings:**
- Morgenstern Y et al (2014) Properties of artificial neurons that report lightness based on accumulated experience
- Purves D, Lotto B (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 2

---

### Lesson 12: Empirical Ranking

**Goal:** Formalize how the frequency of occurrence of stimulus patterns determines perceptual quality through empirical ranking.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Stimuli as Recurrent Patterns | Images activate retinal receptors uniquely; simple recurring patterns are key |
| Luminance and Lightness | Nearby surfaces share reflectance and illumination → frequency determines lightness |
| Explaining Complex Patterns | Percentile rank of luminance co-occurrences predicts contrast effects |
| Feedback Loop | Empirical rank → perception → behaviour → survival → enhanced empirical ranking |

**Further Readings:**
- Purves D, Lotto B (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 7
- Purves D et al (2014) How biological vision succeeds in the physical world

---

### Lesson 13: Color

**Goal:** Explain why color vision is an empirical construct — created by brains for biological advantage — not a physical property.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Seeing Color | Hue, saturation, lightness; four primary categories (unique hues); color space |
| Color Constancy | Surfaces keep their apparent color despite illumination changes |
| Color Contrast | Identical spectra appear different in different surrounds (not due to adaptation) |
| Color Explained Empirically | Frequency of occurrence of spectral patterns predicts contrast and constancy |

**Further Readings:**
- Long F, Yang Z, Purves D (2006) Spectral statistics in natural scenes predict hue, saturation and brightness
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 3

---

### Lesson 14: Color Psychophysics

**Goal:** Show how complex colorimetric functions (JNDs, Bezold-Brücke effect) can be predicted from spectral statistics of natural scenes.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Psychophysics | Systematic variation of hue, saturation, lightness parameters |
| Just Noticeable Differences | Complex wavelength discrimination function predicted by spectral frequency data |
| Bezold-Brücke Effect | Hue shifts with intensity: short wavelengths → blue (dim) or green (bright) |
| Empirical Prediction | Co-occurrence of spectral correlates predicts the psychophysical functions |

**Further Readings:**
- Long F, Yang Z, Purves D (2006) Spectral statistics in natural scenes predict hue, saturation, and brightness
- Purves D, Lotto B (2003) *Why we see what we do*, Chapters 5 and 6

---

### Lesson 15: Motion Speed

**Goal:** Explain perceived motion speed empirically — the flash-lag effect and why physical speed cannot be directly measured.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Physical Motion | Speed and direction conflated by 3-D to 2-D projection; visible range ~0.1–175°/s |
| The Problem | Same retinal image sequence from many different 3-D motions |
| Virtual Environment | Simulating moving objects to approximate frequency of image sequences |
| Flash-Lag Effect | Moving stimulus appears ahead of a coincident flash; increases with speed |
| Empirical Explanation | Frequency of occurrence of projected speeds predicts the psychometric function |

**Further Readings:**
- Wojtach WT et al (2008) An empirical explanation of the flash-lag effect
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 6

---

### Lesson 16: Motion Direction

**Goal:** Show how aperture effects on perceived motion direction are predicted by the frequency of occluded line projections.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Apertures | Framing changes perceived direction (circular aperture → orthogonal to line orientation) |
| Circular Aperture | Predicted by frequency of projected directions through circular occlusion |
| Vertical Slit | "Barber pole effect" — horizontal motion appears vertical through a slit |
| Explanation | Minimum line length fills aperture orthogonally; shorter lines more frequent |

**Further Readings:**
- Sung K, Wojtach WT, Purves D (2009) An empirical explanation of aperture effects
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 6

---

### Lesson 17: Object Size

**Goal:** Explain classical size "illusions," the size-distance paradox, and the Moon illusion in empirical terms.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Classical Size Illusions | Ebbinghaus effect — surrounding objects alter perceived size of identical targets |
| Object Sizes in Scenes with 3-D Cues | Perspective, occlusion, aerial perspective, motion parallax |
| Size-Distance Paradox | Background objects with same physical size appear larger due to empirical rank |
| The Moon Illusion | Horizon Moon ranks higher among diminished terrestrial projections than zenith Moon |

**Further Readings:**
- Howe CQ, Purves D (2004) Size contrast and assimilation explained by the statistics of natural scene geometry
- Purves D et al (2014) How biological vision succeeds in the physical world

### Lesson 18: Stereopsis

**Goal:** Offer an empirical alternative to computational theories of binocular depth perception, explaining ocular dominance.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Image vs Anatomical Correspondence | Standard theory matches image points; alternative uses anatomical correspondence |
| Binocular Circuitry | Evolved artificial neural networks match biological circuitry |
| Ocular Dominance | Binocular neurons dominated by one eye; segregation into cortical stripes |
| Relevance to Perception | Frequency of occurrence of binocular differences → empirical depth scales |

**Further Readings:**
- Ng CJ, Purves D (2019) An alternative theory of binocularity
- Marr D, Poggio T (1979) A computational theory of human stereo vision
- Purves D, Lotto RB (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*, Chapter 5

## Part V — Linking Objective and Subjective Domains

### Lesson 19: Stimuli and Behavior

**Goal:** Reconsider what stimuli really are, and how physiologists and psychologists approach the link between input and output differently.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| What Are Stimuli? | Pre-neural filters alter energy; higher-order processing with feedback loops |
| Behaviours as Understood by Physiologists | Focus on neural mechanisms, reflexes, motor control |
| Behaviours as Understood by Psychologists | Focus on subjective reports, psychophysics, cognition |
| The Common Strategy | Both fields seek to link stimuli to responses; empirical framework unifies them |

**Further Readings:**
- Purves D et al (2018) *Neuroscience*, 6th edition
- Kandel ER et al (2021) *Principles of Neural Science*

---

### Lesson 20: Associations

**Goal:** Show how associations wrought by evolution, lifetime learning, and culture shape the link between stimuli and behaviour.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Associations Wrought by Evolution | Innate responses refined over geological time; species-specific behaviours |
| Associations Wrought by Lifetime Learning | Conditioning (Pavlov, Skinner); practice and repetition |
| Associations Wrought by Culture | Language, social norms, tools — cultural evolution as rapid adaptation |

**Further Readings:**
- Pavlov IP (1927) *Conditioned reflexes*
- Skinner BF (1938) *The behavior of organisms*

---

### Lesson 21: Mechanisms

**Goal:** Explore the neural mechanisms — plasticity, short-term and long-term changes, reward — that implement empirical learning.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Neural Plasticity | The ability of neural connections to change with experience |
| Short-Term Changes | Facilitation, depression, potentiation at synapses |
| More Enduring Changes | Long-term potentiation (LTP), long-term depression (LTD), structural changes |
| Reward | Dopaminergic systems; reinforcement signals that guide trial-and-error learning |

**Further Readings:**
- Hebb DO (1949) *The organization of behavior*
- Kandel ER (2006) *In search of memory*

---

### Lesson 22: Reflexes

**Goal:** Examine whether all behaviour is ultimately reflexive, and consider the counterarguments.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Behavioural Responses as Reflexes | Sherrington's reflex arc; stimulus → processing → response |
| Is All Behaviour Reflexive? | Voluntary action, creativity, free will — apparent counterexamples |
| Counterarguments | Deterministic underpinnings; complexity vs true novelty; illusion of choice |

**Further Readings:**
- Sherrington CS (1906) *The integrative action of the nervous system*

## Part VI — Other Theories

### Lesson 23: Feature Detection

**Goal:** Critically examine the feature-detection theory of neural function — from Hubel & Wiesel to "grandmother cells."

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Feature Detection | Neurons as detectors of edges, orientations, motion — Hubel & Wiesel |
| Neurons Responding to More Specific Stimuli | Face cells, place cells, increasingly specific "features" |
| Perception in Monkeys | Inferotemporal cortex lesions impair object recognition |
| Back to Sherrington | Feature detection as a modern reflex arc; limitations of this framework |

**Further Readings:**
- Hubel DH, Wiesel TN (1962) Receptive fields, binocular interaction and functional architecture
- Gross CG (2002) Genealogy of the "grandmother cell"

---

### Lesson 24: Statistical Inference

**Goal:** Evaluate the Bayesian approach to perception and explain why it falls short compared to the wholly empirical framework.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Statistical Inference | Using probability to infer the most likely source of a stimulus |
| Bayes' Theorem | Prior probability × likelihood → posterior probability |
| The Problem with Bayesian Approaches | Requires a prior (unknowable for biology); still assumes a computable world |
| Empirical Ranking vs Bayesian Inference | No computation needed; experience directly shapes perception without priors |

**Further Readings:**
- Knill DC, Richards W (1996) *Perception as Bayesian inference*
- Purves D et al (2011) *Why we see what we do redux*

---

**Goal:** Consolidate the book's argument: brains do not compute — they operate empirically, and this explains perception and behaviour.

| Topic | Details |
|-------|---------|
| In Brief | Recap of the empirical framework across all domains |
| Key Conclusions | Animals cannot measure reality; perception is shaped by frequency of occurrence |
| Implications | For neuroscience, AI, philosophy of mind |

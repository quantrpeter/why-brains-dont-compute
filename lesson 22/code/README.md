# Lesson 22: Reflexes

Sherrington's reflex arc describes behavior as stimulus → processing → response. The question of whether all behavior is reflexive raises counterarguments about voluntary action and creativity, but deterministic underpinnings suggest that even "voluntary" behavior may rest on causal chains.

## Key Concepts

### Sherrington's Reflex Arc
Sherrington characterized the basic unit of behavior as the reflex arc: a stimulus elicits neural processing, which produces a response. The reflex is automatic, stereotyped, and does not require conscious deliberation. Stretch reflexes, withdrawal from pain, and other innate responses fit this model. Sherrington's framework influenced neuroscience by emphasizing the sensorimotor loop as the building block of behavior.

### Is All Behavior Reflexive?
If reflexes are the fundamental units, is all behavior—including voluntary action, creativity, and "free will"—ultimately reflexive? The question divides those who see behavior as a chain of reflexes (perhaps highly complex and modifiable) from those who posit a qualitatively different kind of causation, such as an uncaused "will" or non-physical mind.

### Counterarguments and Deterministic Underpinnings
Counterarguments to the reflex view appeal to voluntary action (we feel we choose), creativity (novel outputs), and free will. Yet deterministic accounts suggest that voluntary behavior may be the outcome of prior causes—genetic, developmental, and experiential—that we do not introspect. Complexity and unpredictability do not imply true novelty; they may reflect the vast number of interacting factors. The empirical framework is compatible with determinism: associations are built by causes, and behavior is the result.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does Sherrington's reflex arc capture the stimulus–behavior link, and where might it fall short?
2. If voluntary action feels different from reflexes, does that mean it has a different kind of cause?
3. How might the empirical framework accommodate both "reflexive" and "voluntary" behavior without invoking non-physical causation?

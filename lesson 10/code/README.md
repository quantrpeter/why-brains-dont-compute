# Lesson 10: Angles

Acute angles are overestimated and obtuse angles underestimated. Laser-scanned natural scenes reveal that 90° projections are least frequent—right-angle sources require larger planar surfaces, which are rarer. Empirical ranking predicts the psychophysics.

## Key Concepts

### Systematic Angle Misestimation
Perceived angles deviate systematically from physical measurements: acute angles appear larger than they are, obtuse angles smaller. The 90° angle is a special case—it is perceived most accurately, or serves as a reference. These effects are robust across observers and conditions.

### Frequency of Angle Sources in Nature
Laser scanning of natural scenes shows that 90° projections are least frequent. Right-angle sources (e.g., corners of rooms, edges of boxes) require planar surfaces meeting at 90°; such configurations are rarer than acute or obtuse angles in natural geometry. The empirical rank of projected angles—how often each occurs—predicts the psychophysical data.

### Alternative Explanations Rejected
Eye anatomy, lateral inhibition, and other physiological accounts have been proposed to explain angle perception. None are supported by evidence. The empirical framework, by contrast, predicts the systematic misestimation from the statistics of natural scene geometry without invoking special mechanisms.

## Discussion Questions

1. Why are 90° angle projections less frequent in natural scenes than acute or obtuse angles?
2. How does empirical ranking explain both overestimation of acute angles and underestimation of obtuse angles?
3. What would constitute evidence against the empirical explanation of angle perception?

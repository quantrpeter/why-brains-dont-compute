# Lesson 2: Objective and Subjective Reality

There is a fundamental gap between physical (Newtonian) reality and perceived (subjective) experience. Animals cannot measure the environment directly, and perception exists in our heads—not in the physical world.

## Key Concepts

### Newtonian Reality vs Perceived Reality
Classical physics provides a frame for objective measurement: deterministic, measurable quantities. Biological agents, however, lack instruments to measure the world. What we perceive is not a direct readout of physical reality but a construction shaped by evolution and experience. Berkeley's insight—that perception exists in our heads—remains central to understanding this gap.

### The Inverse Problem
The same retinal projection can arise from infinitely many different physical configurations. Stimulus sources are conflated: the brain receives ambiguous input and cannot uniquely invert it to recover the "true" state of the world. This underdetermination is why perception cannot be a simple computation of physical reality.

### Discrepancies Between Measurements and Perceptions
Systematic mismatches exist between what instruments measure and what we perceive. Retinal projection, for example, does not map one-to-one onto perceived size, distance, or orientation. These discrepancies are not bugs but features—perception reflects the statistics of past experience, not the physics of the present moment.

## Discussion Questions

1. If animals cannot measure the environment, how do they succeed at navigating and surviving in it?
2. What does Berkeley's insight that "perception exists in our heads" imply for the study of consciousness?
3. Could non-biological machines ever perceive the world the way we do? Why or why not?

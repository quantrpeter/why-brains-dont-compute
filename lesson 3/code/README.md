# Lesson 3: Algorithms

The history of algorithmic computation spans from the abacus to modern electronic computers. Understanding this lineage clarifies what computation is—and what it is not—in relation to brains.

## Key Concepts

### From Abacus to Electronic Computers
Mechanical computation began with the abacus; Pascal's calculator, Babbage's Difference Engine, and Ada Lovelace's vision of programmable machines followed. Turing's abstract model of computation and Boole's logical algebra provided the theoretical foundation. Electronic realization came with Colossus, ENIAC, transistors, and integrated circuits, leading to the computers we know today.

### Turing Machines and Boolean Algebra
Alan Turing defined an abstract model of computation—a machine that reads symbols, follows rules, and writes output—that remains the standard for what is "computable." George Boole's algebra of logic underpins all digital circuits: AND, OR, NOT operations that compose into any computable function. Together, these frameworks define algorithmic computation.

### Moore's Law and Modern Computation
The exponential growth in transistor density (Moore's Law) enabled minicomputers (PDP-11), Cray supercomputers, and the personal computing revolution. Computation became faster, cheaper, and more pervasive. This success reinforced the intuition that complex problems—including intelligence—might be solved algorithmically.

## Discussion Questions

1. How does Turing's abstract model of computation differ from how biological nervous systems might process information?
2. Why did mechanical and electronic computation develop along algorithmic lines rather than empirical ones?
3. What limits might Moore's Law impose on the computational metaphor for brains?
